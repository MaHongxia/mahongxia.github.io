<h2 style="margin-top:40px;">Working Papers</h2>

<div class="paper-item">
  <div class="row">
    <a href="#" class="paper-title">Focality Dynamics and Platform Competition</a>
    <span class="paper-coauthors">with  <em>draft available upon request</em></span>
  </div>

  <details class="paper-details">
    <summary>Abstract and conferences</summary>
    <div class="paper-details-content">
      <p><strong>Abstract:</strong><br>
      We study how consumers beliefs evolve over time shapes the dynamic competition between two vertically differentiated platforms in the presence of network effects, using the notion of partial focality. We introduce a tractable model of focality updating process with exponentially weighted updating, governed by the persistence of the history. We capture asymmetries in platforms’ focality through both the initial focality level and the persistence of the focality advantage. We show how focality dynamics shape market efficiency, consumer surplus, and the comparative statics with respect to platforms’ discount factors.
Finally, when platforms can make initial-stage marketing investments that shift either the initial focality level or the persistence parameters, we show that the high-quality platform always has a stronger incentive to invest than the low-quality platform.
      </p>

      <p class="conference-info">
      Conferences and talks: ABFER (scheduled, 2026), MFA (2026), 38th Australasian Finance and Banking Conference (2025)
      </p>

<div class="paper-item">
  <div class="row">
    <a href="https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4764605" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer" class="paper-title">
      How Network Effects Shape Firm Entry Strategies
    </a>
    <span class="paper-coauthors">
      with Lisa De Simone (UT Austin), Daniel Rabetti (NUS)
    </span>
  </div>

  <details class="paper-details">
    <summary>Abstract and conferences</summary>
    <div class="paper-details-content">
      <p><strong>Abstract:</strong><br>
      There is a common presumption that network effects intensify firms' race to enter first by strengthening incumbency advantage. We study this idea in an infinite-horizon model in which two vertically differentiated firms choose whether and when to enter a growing market. Incumbency advantage arises endogenously from \emph{consumer beliefs} in the post-entry pricing game: whenever multiple equilibria exist, consumers coordinate on a \emph{focal} firm.
When focality is locked in to the first entrant, network effects indeed exacerbate inefficiently early entry. By contrast, when focality adapts to recent market outcomes, network effects can mitigate premature entry and improve efficiency for sufficiently \emph{patient} firms by weakening the low-quality firm's preemption incentive relative to that of the high-quality firm. Surprisingly, when firms are sufficiently patient and market growth is sufficiently rapid, adaptive focality can tilt the entry race toward the high-quality firm even more strongly than focality that always favors the high-quality firm.
      </p>
      
      <p class="conference-info">
      Conferences and talks: APIOC(2024)
      </p>
    </div>
  </details>
</div>
